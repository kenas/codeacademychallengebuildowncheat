HTML - stands for HyperText Markup Language

HTML is like the structure of the book itself. It's the backbone that organizes everything. Just as the book's structure decides where chapters, paragraphs, and pictures go, HTML determines where the content, pictures, headings, and links will be placed on the webpage. HTML is the framework that gives order and meaning to the story, setting the stage for CSS (the illustrations) to make it visually appealing.

Tags Descriptions
<!DOCTYPE html> as the book's table of contents or the cover page description. It tells the browser about the type of document it's going to read, just like the table of contents gives an overview of what chapters are in the book. It sets the stage for the browser, saying, "Hey, get ready to read an HTML5 book!" It's like the first page of our book, providing the browser with information about the structure of the content it's about to display. 
<header>
 as the title of the book. It's at the top of the webpage and usually holds important info like the title of the story (the website's name) and maybe some chapter titles (like links to different parts of the site). It's like the big, bold title at the top of each page n a book that tells you what the page is all about! 
<title>
 is like the title of the book. It appears at the top of the browser tab and tells readers (or in this case, users) what the page is all about. It's the name of our book that appears on the cover, helping people know what they're reading
</title> is like closing the cover of the book. It encapsulates the title, signaling the end of the title declaration within the HTML document. It's a closing tag that tells the browser that the title content is complete and everything within the <title> tags represents the title of the webpage. 
</header>
 is like the end of the title page. It finishes off the top part of the webpage where all the important introductory info is stored. 
<body>
 is where the story really happens in our web book. It's like all the pages filled with the adventure, pictures, and words. Everything you see and read on a webpage, from the text to the images and even the fun stuff like videos and games, happens inside the <body>. It's where the exciting part of our online story unfolds! 
<a href=""></a>
 is like a magical portal or a hidden pathway within the story. It's an anchor or a link that transports readers (or users) to another part of the same book or even to a completely different book (webpage) when clicked. Just as a magical portal in a book takes you to a different realm or adventure, <a href=""> takes you to a different part of the story or even to a different webpage on the internet when you click on it. 
<img>
 is like an illustration or a picture within our book. It's a visual element that brings the story to life by displaying images. Just as illustrations in a book provide visual context to the story, <img> in HTML displays images on the webpage, helping to convey information or enhance the narrative with visuals. Each <img> tag represents a different image, just as each illustration in a book depicts a different scene or concept. 
</body>
 is like the end of the main story. It tells the computer, "Okay, that's the last page of the adventure!" 
<footer>
 is like the end of a book where you find the closing remarks or a cool note from the author. It's at the bottom of the webpage and often has things like contact info, copyright details, or links to other important pages. Just like the end of a story where you might find the author's final words or a sneak peek at the next adventure, <footer> wraps up our web page adventure! 
</footer>
 is like the final note at the end of the book. It's the closing section that wraps up the webpage, providing last-minute info or links. 
</html>
 is like closing the cover of the book. It tells the computer that the whole webpage is done. It's like saying, "That's the end of our website!"

CSS - stands for Cascading Style Sheets

CSS is like the artist who paints illustrations in our book. It doesn't tell the story (like HTML does) but makes everything look awesome! Just like an artist adds colors, designs, and styles to the pages, CSS adds colors, fonts, layouts, and decorations to our webpage. It's what makes the webpage beautiful and captivating, making sure everything looks just right, like illustrations complementing a story in a book.

Selectors Properties Values Descriptions 
p
 
font-family:
 
Arial, Helvetica

a

10px, 2em
 Adds space inside the links characters share 
ul

block, inline, inline-block, flex, grid
 Determines how lists (like chapters) are displayed 
li

left, right, center
 Aligns text within list items (like chapter titles) 
img
 
width:
 
 100px, 50%, 50px,
 Sets the width of illustrations in the book 
input

2px, 2em, 3rem
 Determines the height of special input fields 
button
 
border-radius:
 
5px, 10px
 Creates rounded corners for buttons in the story 
p
 
background-color:
 
red, #00ff00, hsl, hsla, rgb, rgba
 Sets the background color for key parts of the story
